Departmental Response to the 2024–25 Academic Senate Program Review

UCLA Department of Ecology and Evolutionary Biology

Author

Michael Alfaro, Professor and Chair

Published

March 1, 2026

I. Introduction

The Department of Ecology and Evolutionary Biology conducted its self-study during the 2023–24 academic year and submitted the final report in November 2024. A site visit from the Senate Committee took place on April 8–9, 2025, and the Review Team Report was approved by the Academic Program Review Committee, Graduate Council, and Undergraduate Council in May 2025. We received the report in Summer 2025 and have been working on the issues it raises since that time.

We are grateful to the review committee—internal reviewers Michael Cohen and Catherine Cahill, and external reviewers David Ackerly and Kelly Zamudio—for the care and thoroughness of their evaluation. The committee’s report is both candid and fair. It acknowledges the significant challenges EEB has faced over the past decade, recognizes the progress the department has made under its current leadership, and offers constructive recommendations that align closely with the department’s own priorities. We were particularly appreciative of the external reviewers’ assessment that “the Department of Ecology and Evolutionary Biology at UCLA has a distinguished history as a nationally ranked department spanning critical disciplines of the life sciences” and their emphasis that “sustaining and renewing the excellence of EEB is essential to UCLA’s pre-eminence in the life sciences.”

Preparing for this review, and responding to it, has been a productive process for us. The self-study itself grew out of faculty retreats in 2022 and 2023, blue-sky thinking sessions, surveys of students and staff, and extensive faculty discussion. The review report has given us an external perspective that has sharpened our thinking about priorities. Below, we respond to each of the four recommendations to the Department Chair. As we detail, several of these recommendations address work that was already well underway at the time of the site visit, and in every case we have made substantial progress since the report was issued.

II. Responses to Recommendations to the Department Chair

Recommendation 1

Initiate a comprehensive Academic Strategic Planning Process for the department, with input from all relevant department constituencies, focused on curriculum innovation and pedagogy. Graduate education, field research courses, and the undergraduate Biology major, should be prioritized.

Response:

We agree with the committee that the department needs a comprehensive strategic planning process, and we have initiated exactly that. On December 6, 2025, the department held an all-day faculty retreat at the La Kretz Garden Pavilion—the first such retreat since the strategic planning retreat held at the start of my tenure as Chair in 2018. The retreat was designed to generate concrete action plans across the full range of issues identified in the self-study and the review report.

The retreat was organized around three sessions spanning the committee’s areas of concern. Session 1 focused on undergraduate programs, with breakout groups addressing the Biology major (including the potential for concentrations and a pre-health/non-pre-health split), the EBE and Marine Biology capstone majors (including alternative pathways and the future relationship between these majors), and AI in the classroom. Session 2 focused on graduate programs, with breakout groups on the size and scope of the PhD program, time-to-degree and faster completion, the MS program, and mentoring. Session 3 addressed strategic growth, including revenue generation, alignment with campus-wide initiatives, communicating our work to broader audiences, strengthening research synergies, junior faculty priorities, and faculty promotion and support.

Each breakout group produced a structured action plan with items organized by timescale—immediate (this year), soon (this year and next), and longer-term. Several key outcomes from the retreat are already being implemented:

Undergraduate curriculum. The Undergraduate Curriculum Committee has been charged with four action items from the retreat: (1) creating a motion to change the name of the EBE major to EEB; (2) following up on Winter/Spring 2025 discussions about expanding pathways to the EBE and Marine Biology majors through alternatives to the field quarter capstone requirement; (3) reviewing undergraduate survey results to identify additional steps to increase interest in our capstone majors; and (4) exploring concentrations within the Biology major. The alternative pathways proposal—which would create a coursework-only (non-honors) track alongside an honors pathway for students completing field or mentored research experiences—was first presented to the Executive Committee in February 2025, before the site visit, and continues under active development.

Graduate program. The retreat produced action items on several fronts. Faculty agreed to incentivize co-advising arrangements in the current admissions cycle, to develop a vote on the future of the MS and Evolutionary Medicine MS programs, and to establish a Graduate Program Efficiency and Expectations Committee (GPEEC) charged with restructuring expectations and timelines for degree completion. The graduate curriculum committee has been repurposed for this work in the current academic year. Separately, the mentoring policy (described under Recommendation 2) and a new PhD Exit Seminar requirement are both moving toward faculty votes.

Field program sustainability. In January 2026, I appointed a faculty committee, chaired by the Vice Chair for Undergraduate Education, to conduct a comprehensive review of the field quarter programs. This committee—which includes faculty with direct FBQ/MBQ teaching experience, faculty from other areas, and staff—is charged with developing a long-term sustainability plan for the field programs. The committee held its first full meeting in February 2026 and is developing multi-year financial projections, and options to present to the full faculty. This is the first time in over two decades that the field programs have been subject to open, faculty-wide deliberation, and represents a direct response to the committee’s call for planning processes that involve input from all relevant department constituencies.

Strategic growth. Retreat breakout groups on revenue generation, campus alignment, and external communication produced concrete action plans. Faculty are exploring new summer course offerings, self-supporting degree programs, strengthened partnerships with QCBio, the Institute of the Environment and Sustainability, and the Institute for Society and Genetics, and improved strategies for fundraising and public communication.

We view the December retreat not as a one-time event but as the launch of a planning process that will continue through this academic year and beyond. The action items are being tracked by the relevant vice chairs and committees, and we anticipate that several will result in formal faculty votes during the current year.

Recommendation 2

Design and implement a formal mentorship training program for faculty who work with graduate students, in collaboration with on campus partners such as CEILS.

Response:

This recommendation aligns directly with what has been one of our most sustained and deliberate initiatives over the past three and a half years. We are pleased to report that we have not only begun this work but have made substantial progress through a multi-phase effort that is now approaching the adoption of enforceable training requirements linked to graduate student funding.

Phase 1: Mentoring compact development and adoption (2022–2024). Beginning in 2022, the Vice Chair for Graduate Studies led a department-wide effort to improve mentoring practices. Working in partnership with UCLA’s Center for Education Innovation and Learning in the Sciences (CEILS) and drawing on the nationally recognized CIMER curriculum (Center for the Improvement of Mentored Experiences in Research), the department held multiple faculty workshops and meetings focused on mentoring strategies and standards. These conversations led to the development of a mentor-mentee compact template—a structured agreement between faculty advisors and their graduate students articulating mutual expectations, communication norms, and professional development goals. In December 2024, the faculty voted to formally adopt mentoring compacts for all first-year graduate students. Under this policy, compacts are private agreements between advisor and student; the department does not collect or store them, but the annual guidance committee meeting form was modified to confirm that a compact has been adopted or reviewed. The review committee noted this initiative positively, and graduate students expressed enthusiasm about the compacts during the site visit.

Phase 2: Minimum mentoring standards (Spring–Summer 2025). Building on the compact adoption, the department moved to what we internally described as “Phase II”—establishing enforceable minimum mentoring standards with real consequences for non-compliance. In June 2025, a draft policy was developed proposing mandatory initial mentor training for all faculty who advise graduate students, periodic renewal training (at least 3 hours over a 3-year cycle), and a critical linkage: faculty who have not completed required training would not be eligible to receive departmental funding for new graduate students. Feedback from CEILS was strongly supportive, with the center’s leadership noting that the policy was “long overdue” and offering to partner on training delivery.

Phase 3: Refinement and implementation (Fall 2025–present). The policy, now titled “EEB Ensuring Mentoring Excellence,” has been refined through consultation with CEILS, the Graduate Programs in Bioscience, and additional campus training partners. Two key developments have addressed faculty concerns about training accessibility: the “Entering Mentoring” training program, previously offered only once per year, will now be offered once per quarter; and additional trainers willing to work with individual departments have been identified to ensure that all faculty can access the required training. The mentoring policy is being finalized for a formal faculty vote, with the expectation that compliance will be required beginning with the next admissions cycle.

PhD Exit Seminar. Alongside the mentoring policy, the department has developed a PhD Exit Seminar requirement—a public presentation where graduating doctoral students discuss their dissertation research before the full department. This initiative has been piloted informally with strong positive feedback and is being put forward for a concurrent faculty vote.

The December 2025 retreat reinforced the mentoring initiative. The retreat’s mentoring breakout group identified additional action items including sharing compact experiences across the faculty, engaging with CEILS mentor-mentee conversation sessions, improving availability of advisors and vice chairs to students, and exploring ways to facilitate co-advising. These shorter-term actions complement the policy framework now moving toward a vote.

The progression from voluntary workshops (2022–2023) to adopted compacts (2024) to an enforceable training requirement linked to funding (2025–2026) demonstrates what we believe is a genuinely faculty-driven escalation of commitment. This is not a top-down mandate; faculty have been involved at every stage—through workshops, compact development, feedback on draft policies, and upcoming votes. We are confident that this initiative, once formally adopted, will create durable incentives for mentoring excellence that persist beyond any individual leader’s tenure.

Recommendation 3

Design and implement a field safety training program for instructors assigned to teach field research courses.

Response:

This recommendation reflects a legitimate concern, and we take field safety seriously. However, it is important to clarify that field safety training at UCLA is primarily the responsibility of a centralized university office—the Field Research Safety Program housed within Environment, Health & Safety (EH&S)—not individual departments. EEB’s response to this recommendation therefore operates within the framework of an existing university infrastructure, while also addressing department-specific needs identified through a concurrent internal audit.

UCLA’s Field Research Safety Program. UCLA maintains a dedicated Field Research Safety Program within EH&S that provides field safety plan review by occupational safety professionals; training programs in wilderness first aid, CPR/AED, epi-pen administration, off-road driving, venomous animal safety, and sexual assault/harassment prevention in field environments; equipment loans including satellite communicators, first aid kits, and AEDs; and specialized consultation for high-altitude, desert, high-canopy, and other challenging field environments. EEB, as the most field-intensive department on campus, has historically been one of this program’s primary users.

Recent disruption. In late 2025, the university’s Field Safety Specialist—who had served as the primary coordinator for field safety plans and training for five years and had been a key partner in our field programs—was laid off from EH&S. This departure created significant concern among our faculty and the broader field research community at UCLA. The Vice Chair for Undergraduate Education immediately contacted EH&S to assess the impact. EH&S leadership confirmed that the Field Research Safety Program remains operational, with the specialist’s responsibilities distributed among other EH&S staff, and that all previously offered trainings and the equipment loaner program continue. While we are reassured that the program persists, the loss of a dedicated specialist who understood the unique needs of ecological and evolutionary biology fieldwork represents a real reduction in the quality of support available. We have communicated this concern to university leadership and will continue to advocate for adequate staffing of this program.

Internal audit finding. Independently of the program review, UCLA’s Audit & Advisory Services conducted an internal audit of EEB (Audit #25-5001), with the final report issued in October 2025. Among the audit’s findings was a recommendation that the department address a “lack of field safety plans”—specifically, that we did not have a centralized system for maintaining field safety plans for our courses and activities. In response, the department has implemented the following corrective actions: (1) created a centralized repository for all departmental field safety plans; (2) developed a Standard Operating Procedure for departmental field safety plans, defining requirements and processes; (3) assigned administrative oversight to the Instructional Lab Manager; (4) notified faculty of the new policy requiring field safety plans for all courses and activities involving fieldwork; and (5) begun collecting and centralizing existing safety plans from current field programs. These corrective actions were reported to the auditors in February 2026.

Ongoing training. Despite the disruption at the university level, the department has already scheduled field-specific training for summer 2026, including sexual assault/harassment prevention training for field environments (being scheduled for June) and CPR/first aid certification for field program participants. Going forward, all field courses—FBQ, MBQ, and any course with a field component—will be required to maintain current safety plans reviewed by EH&S, and records will be centralized in the department’s repository with annual review conducted in collaboration with EH&S.

We want to be direct: the department can and should ensure that all field activities have proper safety plans, that these are centralized and monitored, and that faculty and students are connected to the appropriate university training resources. We have done this. But the design and delivery of specialized field safety training programs is the responsibility of UCLA’s EH&S, not individual departments. We actively use and support this program but cannot independently create the specialized safety training infrastructure that exists centrally.

Recommendation 4

Pilot a study to evaluate teaching labor in the department, with a view to creating policies and procedures that distribute teaching more equitably and transparently.

Response:

This recommendation addresses an issue we had already identified as a priority. In fact, by the time of the site visit in April 2025, we were already well into the process of developing formal teaching expectations. Two months later, in June 2025, the faculty voted to adopt seven policies that collectively define, for the first time in the department’s history, what constitutes a standard teaching load and how teaching contributions are tracked.

The seven policies, all of which passed with strong faculty support (13–15 yes votes out of 17 valid ballots), establish the following:

  1. Standard teaching load: Three courses per year—one large undergraduate service course (typically 80+ students), one smaller undergraduate specialty course, and one graduate course. FBQ/MBQ are recognized as substantial service courses despite lower enrollments.
  2. Co-instruction credit: Credit is proportional to instruction—co-instructors receive 50% credit and must teach an additional course to reach 100%.
  3. Minimum unit threshold: Courses counting toward undergraduate teaching are generally expected to be 4 units or more, though exceptions are possible in exchange for significant service activities.
  4. Graduate sections: Graduate sections of undergraduate courses count toward graduate teaching expectations but do not reduce undergraduate teaching obligations.
  5. Sabbatical release: A structured framework specifying which courses are released by one-quarter, two-quarter, and full-year sabbaticals.
  6. Other teaching release: A parallel framework for service-related and family/medical teaching releases, with Chair flexibility for individual circumstances.
  7. Tracking teaching contributions: EEB will calculate 2- and 3-year rolling averages of student enrollment and student credit hours for each instructor, with the methodology accounting for sabbaticals and teaching releases so that leave does not artificially lower averages.

These policies are not merely aspirational. They provide the Chair with a transparent, faculty-endorsed framework for making course assignments and evaluating teaching equity. The rolling averages, in particular, give the department a quantitative tool for identifying and addressing imbalances—the “pilot study” the committee recommended is, in effect, now built into routine departmental operations.

Beyond the teaching expectations vote, future meetings are planned to address the intensive field courses so that the effort put forth in these classes can be understood in light of the new teaching policy.

We recognize that adopting policies is only the first step. The harder work is implementation—actually using these tools to make assignments more equitable, communicating the rationale for decisions, and building faculty confidence that the system is fair. We are committed to this.

III. The December 2025 Faculty Retreat

Because the retreat is referenced throughout the responses above, we provide a brief summary here. The retreat was held on December 6, 2025, at the La Kretz Garden Pavilion, with participation from the full faculty. It was organized around three thematic sessions:

  • Session 1: Undergraduate Programs — Breakout groups on the Biology major, the EBE/Marine Biology majors, and AI in the classroom
  • Session 2: Graduate Programs — Breakout groups on program size and scope, time-to-degree, the MS program, and mentoring
  • Session 3: Strategic Growth — Breakout groups on revenue generation, alignment with UCLA campus initiatives, communicating with the broader world, strengthening synergies, and two sessions on promoting and supporting our faculty (with junior faculty participating in dedicated breakout groups)

Each group produced action plans with immediate, near-term, and longer-term items and identified responsible parties (committees, vice chairs, or ad hoc groups). A wrap-up session aligned the full faculty on priorities. The retreat outputs are being used to guide committee charges and faculty meeting agendas through the current academic year.

IV. Closing Comments

We thank the review committee for a report that is both honest and constructive. The committee understood, correctly, that EEB has been through an extraordinarily difficult period and that the work of rebuilding is far from complete. We share that assessment. At the same time, we want this response to convey something that may not be fully visible from the outside: the degree to which this department has been actively working on exactly the issues the committee has identified—in many cases well before the site visit took place.

The mentoring initiative is in its fourth year. The teaching expectations were voted on before we received the review report. The field safety corrective actions responded to both an internal audit and the review’s concerns. And the December retreat launched a comprehensive strategic planning process that has already produced working committees and concrete action items across the undergraduate curriculum, the graduate program, the field programs, and the department’s broader strategic positioning.

None of this is to suggest that the work is done. We have policies that need to be implemented, votes that need to be held, committees that need to deliver results, and—most importantly—a culture that continues to need deliberate attention and care. The review committee rightly noted that not all members of the department share the same level of optimism about the direction we are heading, and we take that observation seriously. Building trust takes time, consistency, and transparency. We are committed to all three.

We are also mindful that much of what the department can accomplish depends on resources and institutional support. We want to acknowledge that Dean Tracy Johnson has been an exceptional partner in this work. Over the past three and a half years, the Dean has authorized seven new faculty hires for EEB—a remarkable investment that has brought energy, new research directions, and fresh perspectives to the department. This past year, the Dean supported all of our authorized searches, and we were able to complete those hires even as a federal hiring freeze created uncertainty across the university. This level of sustained commitment to EEB’s renewal has been essential to the progress described in this response, and the review committee’s recognition that the Dean’s support should continue is deeply appreciated.

At the same time, we want to be candid about the work that remains. The review committee recommended that EEB be authorized to build its faculty back to its historic norm of 35. We are not there yet, and the needs are acute. Our marine biology program—which anchors one of our three undergraduate majors—still lacks sufficient faculty to sustain the major and its associated field courses. We anticipate the retirement of senior faculty in behavior, conservation biology, and marine biology within the next two years, and these departures will create gaps in areas that have historically defined the department’s identity and public mission. Continued support for faculty hiring, along with the staff positions and leadership support the committee recommended, will be essential to maintaining the momentum we have built. We look forward to discussing these needs at the Progress Review Meeting.
